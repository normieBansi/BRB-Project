Chapter 3: Literature Survey
3.1 Introduction to the Survey
A literature survey within an engineering project fulfills two primary purposes. First, it establishes that the chosen methodologies are grounded in rigorous prior research and that any acknowledged limitations reflect recognized industry challenges. Second, through systematic analysis, it demonstrates that the specific problem addressed by the project remains unsolved, thereby confirming a genuine research gap. This chapter reviews literature across five thematic areas forming the intellectual foundation of the BRB-NGFW system. These areas include the evolution of firewall technology, research trends in Next Generation Firewalls, machine learning based intrusion detection systems, the application of the Isolation Forest algorithm for anomaly detection, and the documented limitations of open source firewall platforms. By synthesizing these thematic reviews into a comparative analysis, this chapter identifies five specific research gaps that the BRB-NGFW architecture is designed to resolve. The reviewed literature spans from 1987 to 2024, drawn from authoritative venues including IEEE, ACM, Springer, Elsevier, NIST, and MDPI. The survey is intentionally selective, focusing exclusively on works directly relevant to the architectural decisions of this project.
3.2 Evolution of Firewall Technologies
The academic study of firewall technology began in response to the Morris Worm of November 1988, which infected approximately ten percent of internet connected machines by exploiting vulnerabilities in Unix utilities. This event demonstrated the collective vulnerability of connected systems and catalyzed serious engineering efforts regarding network boundary control. Denning's foundational 1987 paper, An Intrusion-Detection Model, proposed monitoring system activity against profiles of expected behavior derived from audit trails. This conceptual shift from blocking known bad traffic to detecting deviations from known good behavior remains the intellectual ancestor of modern anomaly detection layers. The first generation of packet filter firewalls, comprehensively surveyed by Ingham and Forrest in 2002, were computationally efficient but fundamentally blind to connection context and application behavior. Scarfone and Hoffman's 2009 NIST Guidelines on Firewalls provided the authoritative taxonomy classifying packet filters, stateful inspection, application proxies, and NGFWs. The introduction of stateful inspection with Check Point FireWall-1 in 1994 marked the critical transition to flow level awareness. Liang and Kim's 2022 study analyzed the architectural progression toward single pass NGFW processing, finding that performance advantages were achieved by eliminating redundant per packet overheads. This directly informs the BRB-NGFW sidecar architecture, which seeks to augment stateful inspection without introducing in line processing latency. Neupane et al.'s 2018 comprehensive survey detailed the transition from rule based to context aware enforcement, noting that advanced capabilities are tightly coupled to proprietary hardware in commercial implementations. This developmental trajectory highlights the necessity of the current leap from signature based detection to machine learning based behavioral detection within the open source ecosystem.
3.3 NGFW Research Trends
Academic research concerning next generation firewall technology has concentrated heavily on architectural analysis, performance benchmarking, and machine learning integration. Heino's 2022 study compared the inspection capabilities of leading commercial platforms against encrypted traffic, discovering that widespread TLS 1.3 and QUIC adoption significantly reduced deep packet inspection efficacy. This finding emphasizes that as encryption expands, signature matching becomes less reliable, necessitating feature based approaches operating purely on flow metadata. Song et al.'s 2020 analysis demonstrated that real time inspection is achievable on commodity hardware without specialized ASICs, validating the feasibility of deploying robust analytical pipelines in resource constrained environments. Singh and Singh's 2024 comparative study evaluated protection efficacy against application layer threats, finding that while commercial NGFWs outperformed conventional firewalls, the performance security tradeoff remained significant. The BRB-NGFW sidecar architecture specifically manages this tradeoff by isolating the inference engine on a separate control node. Crucially, Mohile's 2023 market analysis explicitly noted that adaptive threat prevention remains a differentiator exclusive to commercial products, leaving a profound capability void in open source platforms.
3.4 Machine Learning in Intrusion Detection Systems
The application of machine learning to network intrusion detection has generated extensive academic literature. Ahmad et al.'s 2021 systematic review found that while deep learning architectures achieved higher raw accuracy on benchmark datasets, traditional algorithms like Random Forest and Support Vector Machines remained competitive for real time detection due to substantially lower computational requirements. This directly informs the selection of the computationally efficient Isolation Forest algorithm for the BRB-NGFW project. Zhang et al.'s 2022 comparative study demonstrated that models trained on standard benchmark datasets exhibited poor cross dataset generalization. This critical finding motivates the BRB-NGFW strategy of training models exclusively on traffic derived from the actual target deployment environment. Liu and Lang's 2019 review concluded that unsupervised methods are essential when labeled attack data is unavailable or when the threat landscape evolves rapidly, directly supporting the anomaly based approach of this architecture. Khraisat et al.'s 2019 survey identified the false positive rate as the dominant practical barrier to production deployment, emphasizing the need for transparent operator visibility and tunable contamination parameters rather than entirely opaque automated blocking. Finally, Dini et al.'s 2023 overview identified infrastructure integration complexity as a major deployment barrier, a challenge the BRB-NGFW system specifically mitigates through its decoupled REST API orchestration.
3.5 Isolation Forest in Anomaly Detection
Introduced by Liu, Ting, and Zhou in 2008, the Isolation Forest algorithm has become a standard for unsupervised anomaly detection. Rather than modeling normal behavior and calculating distance metrics, the algorithm evaluates how easily an observation can be isolated through random recursive partitioning. Anomalous observations are inherently isolated at shallower tree depths. The algorithm's computational efficiency makes it ideal for real time operational security contexts. Al Farizi et al.'s 2021 review found Isolation Forest consistently competitive with lower overhead than competing unsupervised methods, particularly when utilizing flow level statistical features such as packet rates and protocol distributions. Tao et al.'s 2018 validation on real world network traffic demonstrated strong discrimination between normal and anomalous traffic using feature categories directly analogous to those extracted from OPNsense filterlogs. Cheng et al.'s 2019 comparative study confirmed that Isolation Forest trains and infers substantially faster than Local Outlier Factor algorithms while maintaining comparable accuracy. While He et al.'s 2023 paper rightly raised concerns regarding vulnerability to adversarial feature manipulation, the algorithm's overarching efficiency and unsupervised adaptability firmly justify its selection as the core analytical engine for this project.
3.6 Open Source Firewall Limitations in the Literature
The academic literature addressing open source firewalls explicitly highlights limitations that motivate this project. Kiratsata et al.'s 2022 comparative study found that while pfSense and OPNsense offered excellent packet filtering throughput, their security coverage was strictly limited to signature based detection via Suricata, possessing absolutely no capability for adaptive behavioral detection. Olivera-Ruiz et al.'s 2024 benchmark confirmed OPNsense as a highly performant baseline but similarly noted the complete absence of machine learning integration capabilities. Che et al.'s 2023 architectural analysis examined the OPNsense REST API, confirming it provides sufficient coverage for external alias table management and rule configuration. This finding directly validates the BRB-NGFW sidecar design, proving that the FastAPI control service can successfully orchestrate enforcement through standard programmatic interfaces.
3.7 Comparative Analysis of Detection Approaches
Synthesizing the reviewed literature yields a comparative assessment of five major detection paradigms: stateless filtering, stateful inspection, signature based IDS, commercial NGFWs, and the proposed ML augmented open source firewall. Stateless filtering achieves high throughput but lacks adaptability and zero day coverage. Stateful inspection, the baseline of OPNsense, adds flow awareness but shares these structural limitations. Signature based systems like Suricata provide meaningful coverage of known attacks but remain rigid and require human response loops. Commercial NGFWs deliver broad detection scopes and automated enforcement, but incur prohibitive licensing costs and utilize opaque proprietary models. The ML augmented open source firewall occupies a uniquely advantageous position. It extends detection to statistical anomalies, offers tunable adaptability through retraining, provides conditional zero day coverage, and executes automated enforcement via API. Crucially, it achieves this at zero licensing cost while maintaining absolute interpretability, as every detection decision is directly traceable to specific extracted log features.
3.8 Identified Research Gaps
The literature review reveals five critical research gaps resulting in measurable security deficiencies within operational deployments. First, despite proven efficacy in academic settings, there is no published work demonstrating the integration of a trained anomaly detection model with a production grade open source firewall in a reproducible pipeline. Second, no documented open source mechanism automatically translates detection outputs into active firewall enforcement without human intervention, leaving response latency bounded by human availability. Third, current literature predominantly relies on artificial benchmark datasets that generalize poorly; there is limited guidance on deriving training data directly from the target environment for unsupervised modeling. Fourth, existing literature treats detection and enforcement as isolated concerns, with no published work demonstrating a complete, integrated pipeline from log ingestion to API driven mitigation. Finally, there is no empirical evaluation confirming that flow level Isolation Forest anomaly detection can effectively identify attack scenarios explicitly designed to evade signature based IDS engines.
3.9 Chapter Summary
This survey has evaluated over thirty works detailing firewall evolution, machine learning intrusion detection methodologies, the structural advantages of the Isolation Forest algorithm, and the documented constraints of open source platforms. The comparative analysis confirms that the ML augmented open source firewall architecture provides a distinct, highly valuable capability profile: adaptive, automated detection with full operator interpretability at zero proprietary cost. The five identified research gaps confirm that integrating these capabilities into a cohesive, reproducible system represents a significant and necessary engineering contribution.
